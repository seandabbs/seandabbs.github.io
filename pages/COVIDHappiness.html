How did the COVID-19 pandemic affect happiness?

Scroll on as we explore the question.


What affects a country's happiness, in general?

The World Happiness Report project examined variables that could affect happiness, across many countries and years. In this WHR dataset, we can find which variable is most correlated with a positive affect on happiness.
Hover over one of the bars to see its value and details about the variable.

Now, how did these variables change during COVID?

Let's look at the last few years...

We see that "freedom to make life choices" has the most correlation to positive affect on happiness. "Perceptions of corruption" is the only variable negatively correlated with a positive affect.

One the other hand, we can look at which variable is most correlated with a negative affect on happiness.

"Social support" has the lowest correlation to negative affect, perhaps indicating that a lack of social support can disrupt happiness.


Perhaps surprisingly, most of these variables increased on average in 2020.
Variables such as "social support" and "generosity" could be associated with increased community interactions, which may be a response to the challenges of the pandemic.
The only variable to decrease was "confidence in national government", possibly explained by how citizens viewed their government's response (or lack thereof) to the pandemic.

Summary

We have seen that COVID has certainly impacted aspects of happiness around the world, but maybe not in ways one would expect. There were noticeable trends. Countries with low "social support", "freedom to make life choices", and "confidence in national government" tend to be towards lower positive affect, and are often stark outliers. Certain country groups and regions, particularly Western Europe, stood out as having low perceptions of corruption, and we can see their cluster of lines appears higher in terms of positive affect on happiness. Some of these trends were to be expected, but our earlier exploration of the variables tells a more hidden story.

At least according to the World Happiness Report's data, it would seem that the challenge of COVID has brought people closer together. Having the freedom to make life choices and having social support were some of the most important variables when it comes to happiness, and perhaps some people experienced more of these because of the pandemic.

It is surprising that even in the face of an unprecedented pandemic, people around the world supported each other and their communities.
